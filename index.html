Subject-Verb Agreement: Part 1


Key concepts

A sentence might not have a subject (depends on the context) but it will always have a finite verb. The term ‘finite’ means definite or certain.
It is named so because a finite verb agrees with the subject and conveys mood, tense, number, and person.
This finite verb and the subject of the sentence must always agree in number, otherwise, the sentence will not sound right.
Always remember that Subjects impact verbs and not the other way round.

  • Case 1: Subjects and verbs must have the same number. This is the fundamental rule that underpins the concept.
  • Case 2: The verb agrees with the preceding noun or sentence when using fractions, percentages, and indefinite quantifiers (for example, all, few, many, many, some).
  • Case 2: Sub-Case 1: With a singular or non-count noun or clause, use a singular verb.
  • Case 2: Sub-Case 2: With a plural noun, use a plural verb.
  • Case 2: Sub-Case 3: Use a singular or plural verb with a collective noun, depending on whether you want to emphasise the group as a whole or its individual members.
  • Case 3: When the focus is on the individuals in the group, the plural verb is employed. This is significantly less prevalent.
  • Case 4: The only time when the object of the preposition factors into the decision of plural or singular verb forms is when noun and pronoun subjects like some, half, none, more, all, etc., are followed by a prepositional phrase. In these sentences, the object of the preposition determines the form of the verb.
  • Case 5: When sentences start with “there” or “here,” the subject will always be placed after the verb, so care needs to be taken to identify it correctly.
  • Case 6: Sub-CASE 1: When ‘majority’/’minority’ refer to an undetermined number greater or less than 50%, use a singular verb.
  • Case 6: Sub-CASE 2: When ‘majority’/’minority’ refers to a given percentage, you can use a singular or plural verb.
  • Case 6: Sub-CASE 3: When the ‘majority’ or ‘minority’ refers to a specific group of people, use a plural verb.
  • Case 7: ‘Time’, ‘money’, and ‘distance’ are frequently expressed using a singular verb.
  • Case 8: Adjectives prefixed by ‘the’ and used as plural nouns require a plural verb.
  • Case 9: Expressions with the phrase ‘number of’ are determined by the phrase's meaning.
  • CASE 9: SUB-CASE 1: They use a singular verb to refer to a single quantity.
  • CASE 9: SUB-CASE 2: They accept plural verbs when used as indefinite quantifiers.

New Words!

  1. Diversity: The state of being diverse; the practice or quality of including or involving people from a range of different social and ethnic backgrounds and of different genders, sexual orientations, etc.
    Use: My father loves the rich cultural diversity of the metro cities in this country.

  2. Multilingual: in or using several languages.
    Use: Dictionaries can be multilingual.

  3. Kinship: blood relationship or a sharing of characteristics or origins.
    Use: The survivors of war feel a deep kinship among themselves.
